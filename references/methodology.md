# Serenity Methodology

This reference contains the full research method. Load it when a task needs a complete Serenity-style analysis or sector map.

## Core Idea

Do not buy the obvious AI story. Look for the overlooked but unavoidable supply-chain chokepoint inside a certain multi-year expansion.

The method asks:

- Is the main theme expanding with high certainty?
- Is there a physical, technical, qualification, or supply bottleneck?
- Is a small but critical company positioned at that bottleneck?
- Is the company near an order, qualification, capacity, or production inflection?
- Has the market not fully priced the inflection?
- Can the thesis be closed with supply-chain evidence rather than narrative?

## 1. Only Work On Durable Expansion Themes

Focus on themes with real demand, capex, and production paths over the next 2-5 years. Avoid short-term topic rotation.

Common Serenity-style themes include:

- AI data centers.
- AI high-speed interconnect.
- CPO / silicon photonics.
- Advanced packaging.
- Liquid cooling.
- Cooling and thermal hardware upgrades.
- Memory, power, grid, server supply chain, equipment, materials, and other AI infrastructure bottlenecks.

Principles:

- Do not chase concept labels.
- Do not rely on one-day price action.
- Prefer real capex, architecture migration, standards, customer demand, and production constraints.

## 2. Avoid The Obvious Mega-Cap Winner

The target is usually not the most covered leader. Serenity-style discovery looks for:

- Under-recognized supply-chain nodes.
- Smaller companies with higher earnings elasticity.
- Companies that can re-rate because a bottleneck becomes visible.

Obvious leaders can validate the theme, but they usually do not create the best information gap.

## 3. Find The Supply-Chain Chokepoint

A good chokepoint is hard to bypass.

Signs:

- Few substitutes.
- Long customer qualification cycle.
- Slow capacity expansion.
- High technical barrier or process know-how.
- High customer switching cost.
- Standards, architecture, or physical constraints force demand.

Example chokepoint categories:

- SerDes.
- Retimer.
- CPO lasers and optical engines.
- InP materials.
- High-speed connectors.
- Low-loss materials.
- Liquid-cooling components.
- Advanced packaging equipment, substrates, materials, and process steps.

## 4. Prefer Small But Critical Companies

Look for companies where one new customer, product cycle, or order can materially change the revenue or earnings base.

Prefer:

- Small or mid market cap.
- Small current revenue base versus a large opportunity.
- Technical positioning already established.
- Early customer validation, sampling, or pilot production.
- Limited current market recognition.

Avoid:

- Companies where the relevant product is immaterial to total revenue.
- Companies already priced as the consensus winner.
- Pure concept stories without product/customer/capacity evidence.

## 5. Look For The Order-Eve Moment

The strongest setup is the transition from 0 to 1:

- Technology position is established.
- Customer validation or sampling is underway.
- Small-batch introduction is near.
- Large-scale production is not fully recognized or priced.

Avoid cases where:

- Volume ramp is already obvious.
- Everyone already knows the thesis.
- Valuation already discounts perfect execution.

## 6. Require Supply-Chain Evidence

Acceptable evidence includes:

- Customer qualification, sampling, or certification.
- Order, contract, fixed-point award, or purchase signal.
- Capacity plan, capex, production line, or facility expansion.
- Mass-production timeline or delivery schedule.
- Management guidance with product/customer/timeline detail.
- Industry standard or technical route evidence.
- Competitor bottleneck evidence.
- Upstream/downstream cross-checks.

Reject:

- Pure concept labels.
- Social media heat.
- Unsourced influencer calls.
- Vague AI narrative.
- Anything that cannot be tied to product, customer, capacity, order, route, or timeline.

## 7. Evaluate The Attention Gap

The ideal setup is known by the supply chain before it becomes broad market consensus.

Check:

- Research report count and intensity.
- Whether the key product is in mainstream narratives.
- Institutional ownership and crowding.
- Liquidity, price reaction, funds flow, and social heat.
- Whether the thesis is still an engineering detail or already a market slogan.

Classify:

- `hidden`: evidence exists, market attention is still low.
- `emerging`: specialist attention is rising, broad market is incomplete.
- `crowded`: broad recognition or valuation already assumes success.
- `overpriced`: price has moved beyond available evidence.

## 8. Track Degradation And Exit Triggers

Each thesis must name what would weaken or break it:

- Valuation already discounts order delivery.
- Competitors enter or substitutes become viable.
- Customer switches technical route.
- Gross margin falls despite revenue growth.
- Capacity expansion fails.
- Customer qualification or order timing slips.
- Repeated financing dilutes upside.
- Market reaches consensus before evidence improves.

Use evidence and risk/reward to downgrade or exit a research thesis. Do not use emotional attachment.

## 9. Core Formula

1. Main theme expands.
2. A physical or technical bottleneck appears.
3. A key supply-chain node becomes scarce.
4. A small company controls or participates in that node.
5. Customer validation, order, or production starts to materialize.
6. Revenue and earnings elasticity surprise.
7. Market reprices the company.

## Practical Screening Flow

1. Define theme: name the theme, driver, demand source, time horizon, and capex/cycle evidence.
2. Map chain: list upstream, midstream, downstream, equipment, materials, components, and potential bottlenecks.
3. Screen companies: map each company to its node, revenue base, market cap, product role, and earnings elasticity.
4. Verify evidence: collect customer, order, capacity, capex, route, and timeline evidence.
5. Pull Futu data: quote, valuation, market cap, plates, liquidity, recent trend, and attention proxies.
6. Score and classify: use `references/evidence_schema.md`.
7. Track: define observation, upgrade, downgrade, and exit triggers.
